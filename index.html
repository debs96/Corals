CORALS:
Beauty, Fragility, and the Fight for Survival

“What rainforests are to the land, coral reefs are to the ocean”

- National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration

Often called the “rainforest of the sea”, coral reefs are critical to marine ecosystems, as they provide shelter, food, and breeding grounds for roughly 25% of sea fish species (NOAA, 2019). However, these reefs are facing extreme threats due to climate change, pollution, and other human activities, and as coral reefs round the world become scarce, the need for immediate action has never been more urgent.

Architects of the Reef

Model: Acropora secale

Corals are ancient animals that belong to the phylum Cnidaria. They first appeared in the fossil record over 400 million years ago and evolved into the modern reef-building forms we know today over the last 25 million years. Corals are made up of individual units called polyps, which have simple, soft bodies and possess stinging cells called nematocysts that help them capture food and defend themselves. Most corals are colonial organisms, meaning they live in large groups of interconnected polyps. These polyps secrete calcium carbonate, forming a hard skeleton that provides structure and protection, as seen by the Acropora secale model. Corals are ecosystem engineers, meaning they create habitats that support a wide array of marine life. They play a vital role in the health of our oceans and provide numerous benefits to humans, including food, coastal protection, and sources of new medicines.

The Blue Harvest

Model: Heliopora coerulea

Coral reefs support some of the most biodiverse ecosystems, with many marine creatures depending on coral reefs for protection, food, and reproduction. Small fish and invertebrates, such as shrimps and crabs, often find shelter among coral branches. In the case of Heliopora coerulea, which has a smooth, flat surface, a unique species of sea slug, Doridomorpha gardineri, camouflages itself to avoid predators. Corals have also been known to be highly prized for jewellery and decoration, making it vulnerable to overharvesting. The high demand for blue coral (Heliopora coerulea) in the jewellery trade has led to its overexploitation, and local and international regulations, such as bans on dredging, are crucial for its protection.

Guardians of the Reef

Model: Tridacna squamosa

Tridacna clams play a vital role in maintaining the health and cleanliness of coral reef ecosystems. As filter feeders, they draw in water and sieve out particles like plankton, algae, and organic matter. This process not only provides the clams with food but also removes excess nutrients and debris from the water, improving its clarity and overall quality. This is crucial for the survival of other reef inhabitants, such as corals, that need clear water for sunlight to reach their symbiotic algae. Furthermore, Tridacna clams serve as nurseries for juvenile fish, provide a food source for predators, and their calcium carbonate shells contribute to the reef's structure after their death. Their symbiotic relationship with zooxanthellae, like that of corals, further boosts the reef's productivity through photosynthesis. By fulfilling these various roles, Tridacna clams contribute significantly to the biodiversity and stability of the coral reef ecosystem.
